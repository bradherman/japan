@devonojas has just unveiled a new listening room in Tokyo, created with @karimoku_research. A project that goes far beyond a simple HIFI installation.

This space is part of his Japanese line, developed and manufactured locally in Japan, using Japanese drivers and a production model designed specifically for the Japanese market. And that changes everything.

This is not a system placed inside a showroom. It is a space conceived as an instrument. Inspired by the architecture of a tea room, the project explores the idea of Ma, the interval, the silence, the distance between things. Sound is not simply played. It moves through an environment designed for it.

At the center, a turntable. Around it, speakers born from this collaboration with Karimoku. Precise cabinetry, controlled volumes, a nearly meditative approach to form. The relationship to wood, proportions, floor level seating, all of it shifts the body’s posture and therefore the listening experience. Music is not consumed. It is inhabited.

What makes this especially interesting is the overall coherence. Designed in Japan. Built in Japan. Intended for Japan. In a world saturated with globalized, interchangeable products, this local logic makes sense, culturally and industrially. It reconnects the audio object to a territory, to craftsmanship, to history.

You can clearly feel the influence of jazz kissa, those Japanese listening cafés where silence is part of the ritual. And you still recognize Devon’s obsession with high efficiency, generous volumes, and the physical presence of sound. But here everything is refined. Nothing is demonstrative. Everything is focused on attention.

For those who love vinyl, large wooden enclosures, horn speakers, Japanese design and vintage HIFI culture, this listening room is a strong statement. It shows that high fidelity can still be a cultural gesture, not just a product.
