## Tax-Free Shopping for Tourists

**Important:** As of your trip dates (spring 2026), Japan's current tax-free system is still in effect (the new refund-based system begins November 1, 2026). Under the current system:

- **Minimum purchase:** 5,000 yen per store per day
- **Process:** Present your passport at the register; the 10% consumption tax is deducted at the point of sale
- **Eligibility:** Foreign tourists with "temporary visitor" immigration status
- **Documents:** Passport required; purchase records are now electronically linked to your passport
- **Consumables** (food, cosmetics, etc.): Must spend between 5,000-500,000 yen; items are sealed in a bag and cannot be opened before leaving Japan
- **General goods** (watches, knives, clothing, etc.): Must spend 5,000+ yen; no sealed packaging required; can use items in Japan

**Tip:** Some department stores (Mitsukoshi, Isetan, Takashimaya) offer an additional 5% discount on top of tax-free when you present your passport at their tourist counter.

**Savings vs. US Prices:**
- Japanese retail prices are typically 5-15% lower than US retail before tax refund
- Add the 10% consumption tax refund = **15-25% total savings**
- With the currently weak yen (as of early 2026), savings can reach **25-35%** on some models
- Example: A Grand Seiko SBGA211 (Snowflake) retails ~$6,600 USD in the US; in Japan, it may cost ~$4,200-4,800 after tax refund and exchange rate
- **Some department stores** (e.g., Mitsukoshi in Ginza) offer an additional 5% off with passport
- **Bottom line:** On a $6,000 USD watch, you could save $1,000-2,000 buying in Japan

## Japanese Knives

### Kappabashi Street (Kitchen Town)
Kappabashi-dori is Tokyo's legendary "Kitchen Town" -- an 800m street lined with kitchen supply shops between Ueno and Asakusa. This is the main destination for knife shopping in Tokyo.

#### Tsubaya (つば屋庖丁店)
- **Address:** 3-7-2 Nishi-Asakusa, Taito-ku, Tokyo
- **Hours:** Mon-Sat 9:00-17:45; Sun & holidays 9:00-16:45
- **Nearest Station:** Tawaramachi Station (Ginza Line), 5 min walk
- **Price range:** 5,000-100,000+ yen depending on knife type and steel
- **Why go:** Established 1956. Widest selection on Kappabashi. Lets you handle most knives without asking staff to open a cabinet -- great for comparing weight, balance, and feel. Very experienced with foreign customers. Good starting point before visiting other shops.

#### Kamata Hakensha (釜田刃研社)
- **Address:** 2-12-6 Matsugaya, Taito-ku, Tokyo
- **Hours:** 10:00-18:00 daily
- **Nearest Station:** Tawaramachi Station (Ginza Line), 5 min walk
- **Price range:** 3,000-80,000+ yen
- **Why go:** Third-generation owner Seiichi Kamata is renowned as a master sharpener. Every knife sold gets a perfect edge before it leaves the shop. Excellent for serious knife buyers who value edge quality.

### Tsukiji Outer Market

#### Masamoto (正本)
- **Address:** Tsukiji Outer Market, Namiyoke Dori street edge
- **Hours:** Generally early morning to 14:00-15:00 (closed some Sundays)
- **Nearest Station:** Tsukiji Station (Hibiya Line), 1 min walk
- **Why go:** Making knives since the 1850s, at the Tsukiji market since 1907. Known for exceptional balance and blade strength. Staff speak good English and are proud to demonstrate their knives.

#### Aritsugu (有次) - Tsukiji Branch
- **Address:** Tsukiji Outer Market area
- **Hours:** 5:30-15:00 (closed Sun, holidays, sometimes Wed)
- **Nearest Station:** Tsukiji Station (Hibiya Line)
- **Why go:** Branch of the legendary Kyoto knife maker (see Kyoto section below). Caters to professional chefs at the market.

### What to Look For (Knife Buying Guide)
- **Gyuto** (chef's knife, 210-240mm): Most versatile, Western-style blade. Best all-around choice.
- **Santoku** (165-180mm): Shorter, flatter profile; great for home cooks with smaller cutting boards
- **Petty** (120-150mm): Utility/paring knife; great gift and very portable
- **Yanagiba** (240-300mm): Long, single-bevel sashimi knife; for serious fish preparation

**Steel Types:**
- **Carbon steel (hagane):** Sharper edge, easier to sharpen, but requires more maintenance (will patina/rust if not dried)
- **Stainless steel:** Lower maintenance, still very sharp, better for casual cooks
- **VG-10, SG2/R2:** High-end stainless steels with excellent edge retention

**Price Guidance:**
- Good quality starter knife: 8,000-15,000 yen ($55-100)
- Excellent mid-range: 15,000-40,000 yen ($100-270)
- Premium artisan: 40,000-100,000+ yen ($270-670+)

**Customs & Bringing Home:**
- Knives must go in **checked luggage** (never carry-on)
- Wrap securely in the original box/sheath plus extra padding
- US customs: Declare on your customs form. Kitchen knives are generally allowed. Under $800 total personal exemption, no duty. Over $800, duty is approximately 6.1% plus any applicable tariffs.
- Most shops will provide a blade guard/saya and proper packaging
- Many shops offer **free engraving** of your name in Japanese or English
- Some shops can ship internationally if you prefer (ask about current shipping costs and tariff implications)

## Chopstick Making Workshops

### Option 1: Ginza Chopstick Workshop (Hashikura)
- **Location:** Ginza area, Tokyo
- **Cost:** ~$27/person base + additional charge for premium wood (17 types available at extra cost). Name engraving: +1,100 yen
- **Duration:** 30-60 minutes
- **English:** Staff who speak English are available; guide fee of 1,000 yen included
- **Booking:** Reserve via GetYourGuide, Activity Japan, or Klook. Free cancellation up to 24 hours in advance.
- **What you do:** Choose from 20 types of wood and 2 lengths. Carve your chopsticks using a traditional hand plane, smooth with sandpaper, coat with oil. Optional: name/phrase engraving in Japanese or English. Take home same day.
- **Why this one:** Convenient Ginza location (combine with watch/knife shopping), beginner-friendly, quick and flexible timing.

### Option 2: Mogami Kogei (Kuramae)
- **Address:** 4-37-10 Kuramae, Taito-ku, Tokyo
- **Hours:** Weekdays only (closed Sat/Sun/holidays)
- **Nearest Station:** Asakusa Station (Toei Asakusa Line), 3 min walk; or Kuramae Station
- **Phone:** 03-3851-6540
- **Cost:** Higher-end experience; inquire directly
- **Duration:** ~90 minutes
- **Booking:** Reservations required at least 1 week in advance. Book through Wabunka (wabunka-lux.jp) for English-guided experience with a licensed national guide for interpretation.
- **What you do:** Learn Edo sashimono woodworking techniques from 3rd-generation master craftsman Yutaka Mogami (workshop founded 1912, 110+ years of history). Choose from precious woods, shape with traditional tools, finish with oil. Receive chopsticks in a paulownia wood box.
- **Why this one:** Authentic artisan experience directly with a master craftsman. More immersive and premium. But weekday-only and needs advance booking.

### Option 3: ZEN Craft Workshop (Shinjuku)
- **Location:** Nishishinjuku 7-chome, Shinjuku-ku, 10F
- **Nearest Station:** Shinjuku Station
- **Duration:** ~60 minutes
- **What you do:** Transform wooden sticks into chopsticks using a traditional kanna (Japanese hand plane). Beginner-friendly with step-by-step guidance.

### Recommendation for Your Group
The **Ginza Chopstick Workshop** is probably the best fit: convenient location near other Ginza shopping (Grand Seiko, Seiko Museum), English-speaking staff, easy to book, flexible timing, and quick enough to fit into a packed day. Book for all 4 travelers (Brad, Alyona, Dave, Gail).

## Japanese Knives

### Aritsugu (有次) - Original Kyoto Location
- **Address:** 219 Kajiya-cho, Nishikikoji-dori, Gokomachi nishi iru, Nakagyo-ku, Kyoto
- **Hours:** 9:00-17:00 (last entry 16:30); closed Wednesdays and Jan 1-4
- **Nearest Station:** Kawaramachi Station (Hankyu) or Kyoto-Shiyakusho-mae Station (Tozai Line), each ~5 min walk to Nishiki Market
- **Price range:** 8,000-100,000+ yen
- **What to expect:** The legendary original, founded in 1560 as swordsmith Fujiwara Aritsugu, making swords for the Imperial family. Transitioned to kitchen knives and wood chisels in the Edo period. Now in its 18th generation. Located on Nishiki Market street. Impressively wide variety of knives, pots, and cooking utensils all handmade by skilled craftspeople. Serves professional Kyoto cuisine chefs and home cooks alike.
- **Tip:** This is THE knife shop of Japan's culinary capital. Even if you bought a knife in Tokyo, it's worth visiting for the history and craftsmanship. Can get busy; try going early. The Nishiki Market location makes it a natural stop during a market exploration day.

- **Tip:** Located in the fun Shinsekai neighborhood, so combine with exploring the area (kushikatsu, Tsutenkaku Tower, retro arcade vibes). This is probably the most tourist-friendly knife-buying experience in Osaka.

### Doguyasuji Shopping Street (Sennichimae Doguyasuji)
- **Address:** Sennichimae, between Dotonbori and Den-Den Town, Namba area
- **Hours:** Most shops 10:00-18:00 (varies by shop)
- **Nearest Station:** Namba Station, 5 min walk. Enter through the gate next to Namba Grand Kagetsu Theater.
- **What to find:** ~150 meters of covered arcade with kitchen supply shops -- knives, pots, pans, tableware, and iconic plastic food samples. Osaka's equivalent of Tokyo's Kappabashi, but more compact.
- **Key knife shop:** **Sakai Ichimonji Mitsuhide** -- over 2,000 types of knives from Sakai artisans. Engraving service available. Traditional Japanese, Western-style, and household knives.
- **Tip:** Even if you don't buy a knife, the plastic food sample shops are uniquely Osaka and make fantastic souvenirs.

### Sakai City (Day Trip Option)
If you're a serious knife enthusiast, Sakai (the birthplace of Japanese kitchen knives) is just a few train stops from Namba Station.

#### Sakai Traditional Crafts Museum (堺伝統産業会館)
- **Address:** 1-1-30 Zaimokucho-Nishi, Sakai-ku, Sakai City, Osaka
- **Nearest Station:** Sakai Station (Nankai Line), 10 min walk
- **What to expect:** Despite the "museum" name, this is actually the best general knife store in the region. The knife makers guild sells directly here, so prices are significantly cheaper than retail shops in Osaka. Wide selection of Sakai-made knives.

#### Jikko Cutlery (Open Factory & Showroom)
- **Established:** 1901 in Sakai
- **What to expect:** Visit their open factory to see where knives are made, then browse the showroom
- **Tip:** Call ahead to confirm factory visit availability. Weekdays only for maker visits.

**Note:** Don't visit Sakai maker shops on weekends -- many are closed. If you go, make it a weekday morning.

## Other Osaka Shopping

### Shinsaibashi Shopping Street (Shinsaibashi-suji)
- **Address:** Shinsaibashi-suji, Chuo-ku, Osaka (covered arcade running ~580 meters)
- **Hours:** Most shops 10:00-21:00
- **Nearest Station:** Shinsaibashi Station (Osaka Metro Midosuji Line), direct exit into the arcade
- **What to find:**
  - ~180 stores under a covered roof (rain-proof shopping)
  - Fast fashion (UNIQLO, H&M, Zara) to luxury (Chanel, Gucci, Cartier on adjacent Midosuji boulevard)
  - **Onitsuka Tiger flagship** (largest in Kansai region) -- great for unique sneaker colorways
  - Japanese cosmetics and drugstores (Matsumoto Kiyoshi, Daikoku Drug) at the entrance
  - Don Quijote nearby for everything from snacks to electronics to Mofusand merch
  - Tax-free shopping widely available

The adjacent Amerikamura (American Village) has more independent/streetwear shops. Connects to Dotonbori (food street) at the southern end.

# HAKONE (May 7-9)

Hakone is primarily for relaxation at the ryokan, but there are a couple of shopping notes:

## Hakone Craft & Yosegi Woodwork
- **What:** Hakone is famous for **yosegi zaiku** -- traditional geometric mosaic woodwork. You'll find it as puzzle boxes, chopstick rests, coasters, and decorative items.
- **Where:** Small shops along the Hakone area, particularly around Hakone-Yumoto Station and the Open Air Museum gift shop
- **Price range:** Small items (coasters, chopstick rests) from 500-2,000 yen; puzzle boxes from 2,000-10,000+ yen depending on complexity
- **Tip:** These make excellent, uniquely Japanese gifts that are lightweight and easy to pack
